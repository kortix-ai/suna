# Trust Boundaries

Read this when a concrete image task touches real people, minors, brands, protected characters, named-artist styles, or any evidence-like artifact (screenshots, dashboards, receipts, IDs, invoices, lab results, before/after proof). Resolve the boundary before optimizing the prompt or picking a model.

## Evidence-like imagery

Don't fabricate or imitate anything that could function as real-world proof: screenshots, search results, dashboards, chat logs, receipts, invoices, statements, IDs, passports, tickets, prescriptions, lab results, court or government documents, news photographs, CCTV or body-cam frames, license plates, signed contracts, real third-party logos or partner branding, or before/after "results."

A stylized or labeled-illustrative version is fine **only when the output can't pass as the real thing**. For an ID-style prop, use a fictional jurisdiction and a visible `PROP — NOT A REAL DOCUMENT` mark. For any proof-like request, either decline or convert it into a clearly fictional, visibly labeled illustration.

## Real people

Never place a real person in a deceptive, embarrassing, intoxicated, sexual, criminal-suggestive, in-distress, fake-political, fake-medical, or evidentiary context, or imply they did or said something they didn't — whether by prompt or by reference instruction.

Editorial illustration and satire of public figures are allowed only when the result is clearly non-photoreal, labeled illustrative where needed, and never framed as a real photo, document, screenshot, or news event. For political or public-figure satire, write explicit non-photoreal and illustrative-label instructions — style words alone aren't enough. The output must not be able to pass as a real photograph.

Don't alter a real person's identity attributes, body shape, disability status, age presentation, or likeness — by edit, face blend, or composite — unless the use is benign, consented, and non-deceptive.

Paparazzi, candid, surveillance, and celebrity references need a stated benign, authorized use. Don't identify people in future references, don't assume consent, and don't optimize for a realistic candid/paparazzi look.

"Clearly fictional" means non-photoreal, stylized, or a generic unnamed subject with no recognizable real-person likeness. Renaming is not enough.

## Minors

For real or apparent minors: no sexualized imagery, and no realistic or evidence-like depictions involving violence, crime, intoxication, clinical treatment, weapon handling, or commercial endorsement.

Clearly non-realistic illustration in benign storybook, fairytale, or play contexts — including toy weapons — is fine. If age is uncertain, treat the subject as a minor.

## Brands, characters, and artists

For third-party brands, don't generate logos, packaging, mascots, brand-specific trade dress, endorsement framing, or co-branded lockups unless the user supplies the assets and confirms authorization. Style influence is not brand replication. With no usable assets, offer generic, fictional, or user-owned branding.

Don't depict named copyrighted characters or mascots. Convert them into non-infringing visual attributes — and don't promise to recreate the named character later.

For named-artist styles, translate to concrete attributes — palette, material, line, lighting, composition — and drop the artist's name from the final prompt unless the user holds rights.
