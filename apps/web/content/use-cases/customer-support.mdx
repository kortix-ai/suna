Support is the hardest place to put AI to work — and the most tempting. It's
where product knowledge, engineering context, and billing all collide. A
chatbot bolted onto a help center can recite documentation, but it can't read
the code a customer is hitting a bug in, confirm whether their subscription
actually renewed, or take a safe, scoped action to fix the problem.

So we built one that can. This is exactly how we run our own customer support on
Kortix — every decision, every connection, every guardrail — laid out so you can
build the same thing for your team.

## The problem

Every growing product hits the same wall. Support volume climbs faster than the
team. The questions that matter most are the ones a canned macro can't
answer — "why was I charged twice?", "this button does nothing", "does your API
support X?" Each one needs someone who understands the product, can read the
code, and can check the customer's account. That's a senior engineer's time,
spent on a queue that never empties.

The usual fixes all fall short. A help-desk bot deflects the easy questions and
frustrates everyone on the hard ones. Hiring scales linearly with tickets.
And handing a generic AI assistant real access to your systems is a security
review nobody wants to sign off on.

<PullQuote>
  The goal was never to deflect tickets. It was to resolve the underlying issue
  — safely — before a human ever opens the tab.
</PullQuote>

## What we built

On Kortix, our support inbox in **Plain** is wired to an agent that runs like a
member of the team. Every inbound thread spawns its own isolated session — a
real cloud computer — with scoped reach into exactly the systems a support issue
can touch: the product's knowledge, the codebase, and Stripe. It investigates,
resolves what it can, and stops at a human for anything sensitive.

## How we did it — step by step

<Steps>
<Step title="Connect Plain as the trigger">

We pointed a signed webhook from Plain at the project. Every new thread — or new
customer reply — fires it, and each firing spawns a fresh **session** in its own
isolated sandbox. One customer, one thread, one disposable machine. Nothing
leaks between conversations, and a busy inbox just means more sessions running in
parallel.

</Step>
<Step title="Give the agent the product's knowledge">

Product understanding lives as **skills** and **memory** that ride into every
session: how the product works, our support playbook, the tone we reply in, and
the resolutions that worked before. The agent doesn't guess from a stale help
center — it reasons from the same knowledge our team does, and it gets sharper
every time a thread is resolved.

</Step>
<Step title="Connect the systems an issue can touch">

Through scoped **connectors**, brokered server-side so no raw token ever reaches
the model, the agent can:

- **Search the codebase** — when someone reports a bug, it finds the exact code
  path, checks recent changes, and confirms whether it's already known.
- **Look up Stripe** — plan, invoices, and subscription state, so billing
  questions get answered with facts instead of guesses.
- **Read and reply in Plain** — full thread context in, a resolved reply out.

</Step>
<Step title="Set the guardrails">

Reach without restraint is a liability, so this step matters most. The agent is
**read-mostly by default**: it investigates freely, but every write is scoped
tight. Refunds, plan changes — anything that touches a customer's money or
account — stop at a **human approval gate**. Credentials are encrypted in the
Secrets Manager and injected at runtime, never shown to the model or written to
logs.

</Step>
<Step title="Let each thread run itself">

With that in place, an inbound thread triages, investigates across all three
systems, and either resolves directly or hands off to a human with the work
already done and the context attached. A "why was I charged twice?" becomes a
Stripe lookup with a real answer. A "this button does nothing" becomes a
codebase search that either explains the behavior or files a clean bug report
for engineering.

</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout title="The pattern, in one line" tone="accent">
  Connect the support platform via a **trigger**, give the agent scoped
  **connectors** into the systems an issue can touch, encode the product
  knowledge as **skills** and **memory**, and gate every sensitive action behind
  a human. Then let each inbound thread run itself.
</Callout>

## Guardrails: safe enough for production

Handing an agent your codebase, Stripe, and your support inbox at once is a
security question, not just a product one. It's answerable on Kortix because the
controls are built in, not bolted on:

- **Isolation.** Every thread runs in its own microVM sandbox on its own branch.
  A session can install, run, and experiment to reproduce a bug — and only what
  it's explicitly allowed to send ever leaves.
- **Scoped secrets.** The Plain, Stripe, and GitHub credentials live encrypted in
  the Secrets Manager, injected into the sandbox at runtime, never exposed to the
  model or the logs.
- **Human approval gates.** The irreversible actions — money, account state —
  don't happen without a person clicking approve.
- **Everything is code.** The agent's persona, its skills, and its permissions
  are files in the repo — reviewable, versioned, and changed only through a
  reviewed **change request**, never a hidden dashboard setting.

## Why this is hard to build anywhere else

A help-desk bot can read your docs. It can't safely be handed the codebase,
Stripe, and your support inbox at once — because it has no isolation to contain a
session, no secrets manager to broker the tokens, no approval gates to hold back
the irreversible actions, and no memory to get better with every resolved thread.

Kortix has all four. That's what turns "a chatbot on the help center" into a
member of the support team that actually closes the loop — and it's the same
foundation you'd build your own support agent on.
